Design #1

This haori is inspired by calligraphy scrolls from the Nezu museum, specifically the practice of gold borders on the scrolls. I thought it was incredibly elegant, and I immediately saw it on a coat or blazer, which is what prompted me to create a fashion line for the final project.

The sleeves, in particular, were the perfect place to add calligraphy scroll patterns. Haori sleeves are bold and dramatic. This would make the lines of the scroll patterns fall organically.

While one side of the garment is elegant and refined, featuring the patterns of calligraphy scrolls, the other side is grungier, carrying more of a streetwear aesthetic with the chains and macrame rings. It brings together traditional and modern styles, which is a theme that I enjoy exploring.

Design #2

This haori incorporates my personal liking for suits, especially given the notch lapel. It also leans into simplicity and austerity, with one side plain black and the other side with a simple, unobtrusive pattern.

The actual pattern itself is from a sake bottle, which I thought was a simple yet bold pattern to put on a garment.

Since haoris are a sign of elegance, and sometimes status, I felt it was fitting to use this pattern on a garment made to look like a blazer.

Design #3

Something interesting about haori patterns is that, when laid flat with sleeves tucked back, they can form patterns and art. In Japan, I saw some examples that would unfold into an image of sorts. I knew I wanted to design a haori that could replicate this style.

There was a particular fan in Kyoto that appealed to me. It specifically aligned with the aesthetic of Yūgen, blending a dark blue and black with smattering white pattern. This pattern resembles a crack in the fan, worn from age. It's repaired using silver, which is a nod to Kintsugi.

After getting my own haori in Japan, one of my favorite things to do has been to see how the back comes together. That's why I wanted to use the pattern of the fan in this garment.
