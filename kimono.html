Design #2

This kimono is inspired by Ukiyo-e, seen at the Tokyo National Museum, and calligraphy. The Kanji on the sleeves are drawn as brushstrokes and printed similar to the printing style in Ukiyo-e. There is a specific work of art in the exhibit I saw called A Meeting of Minds by Nick Walker (2025). The painting is of someone holding a bucket, from which a dragon is emerging. This dragon is in gold and silver, a metallic combination that previously didn't appeal to me but somehow seemed to work here.

The Kanji symbol is Iki ("Ki"). It's an abstract concept, overall conveying the concept of "spirit" or "air" Dragons are essentially the embodiment of spirit and air, so it felt fitting to use this symbol with the color scheme of the dragon Ukiyo-e. The lavishly laid chains, also mirroring this color scheme, represent strength and greed, as dragons are known for.

Overall, this garment is a subtle way to convey the meaning and spirit of the dragon without coming forth and saying it outright.

Design #3

One of the hallmarks of this trip was being in Osaka at the same time as World Expo 2025. I enjoyed looking at the buildings and pavillions, taken by the geometric patterns in the architecture.

This kimono is inspired by the geometric patterns of the Serbia pavillion. The lines were too long to go inside, but I loved how the pattern looked like streetwear/armor plates. I wanted to incorporate that design into this kimono, especially because armor and combat are part of the Samurai aesthetic.

The panels are made of a thin, hard material with metallic fabric sewn over them (to prevent clanging). Light enough to encourage movement but firm enough to hold shape. The panels are fastened by sewing them to the garment.
